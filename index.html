Verify these against the COROS app. Numbers above are estimated from MHR 189 using standard COROS percentage bands. The COROS app holds the source of truth, read your six zones from there and trust those values over anything written here. If they diverge by more than 3–4 bpm in any band, the app wins. Important: in the COROS app, set the zone calculation method to Heart Rate Reserve (HRR / Karvonen) rather than percent-of-max. HRR uses both MHR 189 and RHR 51 and gives more accurate zones for trained runners. Percent-of-max alone overestimates the lower zones and compresses the upper ones.

Why this plan uses both threshold and intervals. A 10K is run at roughly 90–95 percent of VO2max for trained sub-43 athletes. That puts it close to two physiological ceilings at once: lactate threshold (how much fast aerobic work can be sustained) and VO2max (the ceiling on aerobic power). Threshold work raises the first, I-pace intervals raise the second. Marathon plans skip intervals because the binding constraint there is durability, not ceiling. For 10K both are binding, so both are trained.

What This Block Must Deliver

  • Volume: rebuild from 40 km/wk to a peak of 56–58 km/wk, capped at historical peak.
  • Threshold ceiling: sustain T-pace from 4:18/km (week 2) to 4:03/km (week 12), a 15 sec/km improvement.
  • VO2max touch: three I-pace sessions (weeks 7, 11, 14) to lift the ceiling without overcooking.
  • Race-pace rehearsal: 3–4 km at 10K race pace inside the long run, weeks 10 and 11.
  • Knee durability: two strength sessions weekly, posterior chain and single-leg dominant, never skipped.

The Layoff Context

Two months out with runner's knee after returning too fast post-marathon. The block treats weeks 1–4 as a rebuild rather than a base phase in the classical sense, which is why threshold work is introduced cautiously (cruise intervals before continuous tempo) and the first I-session is held until week 7. The risk is not the volume itself, it is the rate of change. Hit the volumes shown, do not chase any that get missed.

Protein: 1.8 g/kg = ~160g/day. 25–30g within two hours of any quality or long session.

On the cutting goal: a meaningful caloric deficit during a 10K block targeting a 2:45 PB is risky. Threshold and VO2max adaptations need full energy availability, and the knee history compounds the risk. Cap any deficit at roughly 250–300 kcal/day, hold protein at 1.8 g/kg minimum, and front-load carbs around quality days. If body weight is dropping faster than 0.3 kg/week, the deficit is too aggressive. Stop cutting completely in the final three weeks.

Caffeine Protocol

Standard pre-quality and pre-race: 3 mg/kg = ~270mg, 60–90 min before. Already tolerates caffeine well per intake.

Race day: 4–5 mg/kg ceiling, only if confident from training. Do not introduce a new dose on race day.

The warm-up is not optional for a 10K. A 10K is run close to VO2max from the gun. Going in cold means the first 2–3 km are spent raising HR and breathing rate at the same time as racing, which costs 10–20 seconds that cannot be recovered. The four strides are the bit that matters most.

10K Pacing · Controlled Build

Km 0–2: 4:02–4:05/km. Controlled, do not chase early surges. The bunch will go off fast, let them.

Km 2–7: 3:59/km. Lock the rhythm. This is where the race is run. The first 2km will have averaged 4:02–4:05, so 3:59/km here keeps the cumulative on target for 39:55.

Km 7–9: 3:58–3:59/km if breathing allows. The doubt window. Hold form, drive arms.

Km 9–10: empty the tank. Final kilometre can drop to 3:50/km if there is anything left.

Race Day Fuel · Minimal

A sub-43 minute 10K needs no mid-race fuel. Pre-race breakfast and caffeine cover the energy demand. Carry nothing.

Exception: if the start is delayed by more than 60 minutes from the planned start time, take a 25g gel 30 minutes before the new start.

Daily Flags

  • RHR 5+ bpm above 51 baseline, two consecutive mornings: easy only. Move quality to the next day or drop it.
  • RHR 8+ bpm above baseline: full rest. Reassess in the morning.
  • Knee pain, anterior or lateral, during or after a run: stop immediately. Ice 15 min. Two days off. If still present on return, see a physio before continuing.
  • Under 6 hours sleep two nights in a row: downgrade the next quality session to easy.
  • Low motivation plus general aches 3+ days running: unplanned deload week. Skip quality, hold easy volume, recover.

Session Signals

  • Easy pace drifting 15+ sec/km slower at the same HR over a week: fatigue accumulating. Drop the next quality session, hold easy volume.
  • Cannot hit T-pace in a session: slow by 8 sec/km and call it sub-threshold. Do not push through and do not extend.
  • I-pace reps degrading by more than 5 sec/km from rep to rep: stop the session. The VO2max stimulus is done, more reps just add fatigue.
  • Two long runs in a row where HR drifts more than 10 percent over the run: aerobic base needs more work. Drop intensity, add easy volume.

Missed Sessions

Do not stack. The next session is whatever was already planned, not the missed session bolted on top.

Missed easy run: skip. Move on.

Missed quality: shift one day if Wednesday-to-Thursday is feasible, otherwise skip. Never replace a Friday easy with a quality session because a Wednesday was missed.

Missed long run: if no quality between, shift by one or two days. If four days late or more, shorten the long run by 25–30 percent.

Two or more sessions missed in a week: hold the next week flat (no progression in volume or intensity), reassess.

Race Week Illness

Mild cold, head only, no fever: race goes ahead.

Chest, lungs, or fever: do not race. 10K at threshold on a body fighting infection risks myocarditis. There will be other 10Ks.

Stomach bug 48+ hours out: if fluids have stayed down for 24 hours pre-race and weight is stable, start. Otherwise do not.
